How Are Generation Boundaries Defined?

Generation boundaries are not arbitrary - they're set by researchers based on shared formative experiences. The Pew Research Center, the most widely cited source, uses these criteria:

  • Millennial/Gen Z boundary (1996/1997): People born 1996 or earlier were old enough to remember 9/11; those born 1997 or later generally were not.
  • Gen Z/Gen Alpha boundary (2012/2013): Those born 2012 or earlier remember life before tablets and voice assistants became ubiquitous; those born 2013 or later do not.
  • Gen X/Millennial boundary (1980/1981): Those born 1980 or earlier had analog childhoods; those born 1981 or later grew up with increasing digital technology.
  • Boomer/Gen X boundary (1964/1965): Aligned with the end of the post-WWII birth rate surge.

Gen Z (born 1997-2012) grew up with smartphones and social media. Gen Alpha (born 2013-2025) is growing up with AI assistants, voice-activated devices, and tablets from birth. Gen Alpha will be the first generation to have AI tutors and generative AI as normal parts of their education.

A Zillennial is someone born on the cusp between Millennials and Gen Z, roughly 1993-1998. Zillennials remember life before smartphones but adopted them in their teens. They have traits of both generations - they may remember dial-up internet but are fluent in TikTok.

Following the Greek alphabet naming convention, the generation after Alpha will be Generation Beta, expected to include those born from approximately 2025 to 2039. The name was suggested by researcher Mark McCrindle, who also coined "Gen Alpha."

Gen Z follows the alphabetical naming convention: Generation X was followed by Generation Y (Millennials), then Generation Z. Other proposed names like "iGen" and "Zoomers" exist, but "Gen Z" remains the most widely used term.
