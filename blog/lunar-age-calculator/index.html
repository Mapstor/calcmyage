What Is Lunar Age?

Lunar age (also called nominal age, virtual age, or in Chinese: xu sui, 虚岁) is an East Asian age-counting method based on the traditional Chinese lunisolar calendar. The system operates on two fundamental principles that differ markedly from Western age counting:

  1. You are 1 at birth. Time spent in the womb is counted as the first year of life. At the moment of birth, a baby is already considered to be 1 year old (sometimes described as "1 sui" - 1 岁). This reflects the Confucian value of honoring the prenatal period as part of a person's existence.
  2. Age increases at Lunar New Year. Everyone adds 1 year to their age on the first day of the Lunar New Year (Chinese New Year), not on their individual birthday. This creates a communal aging experience where everyone "ages" together on the same day.

The result is that your lunar age is always 1-2 years higher than your Western age. The exact difference depends on whether your birthday falls before or after the Lunar New Year in a given year.

Lunar Age Formula

Lunar Age = Current Year - Birth Year + 1 (using lunar calendar years)

For a simplified calculation using the Gregorian calendar: if today's date is before the Lunar New Year, use last year's Lunar New Year as the reference. If it's after the Lunar New Year, use this year's. For precise calculations that account for the exact dates, use our age calculator's Korean/Lunar tab.

Understanding the +1 and +2 Difference

The difference between lunar and Western age can be either +1 or +2 years, depending on timing:

  • +1 year difference: Occurs when your birthday falls between January 1 and the Lunar New Year date of the current year. You've already had your Western birthday, but the lunar "age-up" hasn't happened yet.
  • +2 years difference: Occurs for most of the year. The lunar "age-up" has happened (at Lunar New Year), but your Western birthday hasn't arrived yet, creating a two-year gap.

History and Origins of Lunar Age

The lunar age system has roots stretching back over 4,000 years to ancient China. Understanding its history illuminates why this seemingly complex system developed and persisted.

Ancient Chinese Origins (2000+ BCE)

The earliest forms of the Chinese calendar date to the Shang Dynasty (1600-1046 BCE), though astronomical observations began even earlier. The lunisolar calendar was developed to track both lunar phases (important for religious rituals and festivals) and solar seasons (critical for agriculture). According to historical records documented by History.com, Chinese astronomers created one of the world's most sophisticated ancient calendar systems.

The practice of counting age from 1 at birth likely emerged from several cultural factors:

  • Reverence for prenatal life: Confucian philosophy valued the time spent in the mother's womb as the beginning of existence. The nine months of pregnancy was rounded up to one year.
  • Practical record-keeping: In ancient times without precise birth records, tracking individual birthdays was difficult. Having everyone age together at New Year simplified administration.
  • Communal identity: The system reinforced social bonds by creating shared age-cohorts (people born in the same lunar year) who experienced milestones together.

Modern Transitions

Throughout the 20th century, most East Asian countries officially adopted the Western (Gregorian) calendar and international age system for legal and administrative purposes. However, lunar age has proven remarkably resilient in cultural contexts. The most recent major change came in June 2023 when South Korea officially switched to international age for all legal purposes, though Korean age remains common in everyday conversation.

Step-by-Step Calculation Methods

There are several approaches to calculating lunar age, ranging from quick estimates to precise calendar-based methods.

Method 1: Quick Estimate (Most Common)

This method works for everyday purposes and is accurate within the year:

  1. Take your current Western age
  2. Add 1 (for starting at 1 at birth)
  3. Check if today is after Lunar New Year but before your birthday - if so, add another 1

Example: Sarah was born on August 15, 1995. Today is February 6, 2026.

  • Western age: 30 (her birthday hasn't happened yet in 2026)
  • Add 1: 30 + 1 = 31
  • Lunar New Year 2026 is February 17 (hasn't happened yet)
  • Current lunar age: 31 (will become 32 after February 17)

Method 2: Year Subtraction Formula

This formula-based approach is used in many online calculators:

Lunar Age = Current Year - Birth Year + 1

Adjust by -1 if before Lunar New Year and before your birthday falls within the lunar year

Method 3: Precise Lunar Calendar Conversion

For exact calculations (needed for Chinese gender prediction charts), you must convert dates to the lunar calendar:

  1. Convert your birth date from Gregorian to the Chinese lunar calendar (available on timeanddate.com)
  2. Identify the lunar year of your birth
  3. Count the lunar New Years that have passed since your birth
  4. Add 1 for your starting age

Worked Example: Detailed Calculation

Person: Born December 28, 1999

Calculate for: February 6, 2026

  1. December 28, 1999 in the lunar calendar falls in lunar year 4697 (Year of the Rabbit, ending February 4, 2000)
  2. Today (February 6, 2026) is in lunar year 4723 (Year of the Snake, before February 17 Lunar New Year)
  3. Wait - Lunar New Year 2026 is February 17, so we're still in lunar year 4722 (Year of the Snake)
  4. Lunar years passed: 4722 - 4697 = 25
  5. Add starting age: 25 + 1 = 26 years old (lunar age)
  6. Western age: 26 (birthday December 28, 2025 already passed)
  7. Difference: 26 - 26 = 0... but after February 17, lunar age becomes 27 while Western age stays 26

Regional Variations Across Asia

While the basic concept of starting at 1 and aging at New Year is shared across East Asia, important regional differences exist.

China (Xu Sui System)

In mainland China, lunar age (xu sui, 虚岁) is distinguished from Western age (zhou sui, 周岁). The term "xu sui" literally means "virtual years" or "nominal years." While China officially uses Western age for legal documents, healthcare, and government records, xu sui remains important for:

  • Traditional medicine consultations
  • Fortune telling and astrology
  • Family celebrations and ancestral rituals
  • Chinese gender prediction charts
  • Informal social contexts, especially among older generations

South Korea (Until June 2023)

Korea's traditional age system (Korean age, 한국 나이) operated similarly to Chinese lunar age, but with one key difference: age increased on January 1 of the Gregorian calendar, not at Lunar New Year. This made calculation simpler but created the same 1-2 year gap with international age.

In June 2023, South Korea officially adopted international age for all legal and official purposes, making it one of the last major countries to standardize. However, Korean age persists in everyday conversation, social hierarchies, and cultural contexts. Learn more in our Korean Age Calculator guide.

Vietnam (Tuổi Mụ System)

Vietnamese traditional age counting (tuổi mụ, meaning "counted age" or "nominal age") follows the Chinese lunar calendar closely. Age increases at Tết (Vietnamese Lunar New Year), which coincides with Chinese New Year. Vietnamese people commonly know both their tuổi mụ (lunar age) and tuổi dương (Western age).

Japan (Kazoedoshi System)

Japan's traditional age system (kazoedoshi, 数え年) was officially replaced with Western age in 1950, making Japan the earliest major East Asian country to fully transition. Today, kazoedoshi is used almost exclusively in Shinto shrine rituals and certain traditional ceremonies. The term literally means "counted years."

How the Lunar Calendar Works

Understanding lunar age requires understanding the lunisolar calendar that underpins it. The Chinese calendar is not purely lunar (like the Islamic calendar) but is a lunisolar calendar that incorporates both lunar phases and the solar year.

Key Features of the Chinese Lunisolar Calendar

  • Months follow the Moon: Each month begins on the new moon and lasts 29 or 30 days. A lunar month (synodic month) is approximately 29.53 days.
  • 12 months per year normally: A regular lunar year has 12 months, totaling 354 or 355 days - about 11 days shorter than the solar year.
  • Leap months: To stay aligned with the seasons, a 13th month (leap month or intercalary month, 闰月) is added roughly every 2-3 years. This keeps festivals like Chinese New Year in late January to mid-February.
  • Year begins at Lunar New Year: The first day of the first lunar month, which falls between January 21 and February 20 on the Gregorian calendar.

The Metonic Cycle

The Chinese calendar uses the Metonic cycle - a 19-year period during which there are 235 lunar months, almost exactly equal to 19 solar years. This means that after 19 years, the moon phases repeat on approximately the same dates. The calendar inserts 7 leap months during each 19-year cycle to maintain seasonal alignment. This astronomical precision, documented by National Geographic, makes the Chinese calendar one of the most sophisticated traditional timekeeping systems ever developed.

Ancestral Worship and Rituals

During important Chinese festivals like Qingming (Tomb Sweeping Day) and the Ghost Festival, lunar age determines seniority and proper ritual behavior. The oldest family member (by lunar age) typically leads ceremonies, and seating arrangements at formal dinners follow lunar age order. This creates a clear social hierarchy that extends even to cousins and extended family.

Marriage Compatibility

Traditional Chinese matchmaking considers the lunar ages and zodiac signs of both partners. Certain age combinations are considered auspicious or inauspicious based on:

  • Zodiac compatibility: Some animal signs are believed to harmonize (Dragon and Rat) while others clash (Tiger and Monkey)
  • Age gap analysis: Certain age differences are considered more fortunate than others
  • Five elements theory: Each year is associated with an element (Wood, Fire, Earth, Metal, Water) that interacts with partners' elements

While modern Chinese families increasingly ignore these traditions, awareness of them remains widespread, and some families still consult fortune tellers who use lunar age calculations to assess compatibility.

The Chinese Gender Prediction Chart

One of the most well-known uses of lunar age is the Chinese Gender Prediction Chart (also called the Chinese Birth Chart or Chinese Gender Calendar). This traditional chart claims to predict the gender of an unborn child based on the mother's lunar age at conception and the lunar month of conception.

Origins and History

According to legend, this chart was discovered in a royal tomb near Beijing over 700 years ago, dating back to the Qing dynasty. Some accounts claim it was found in the Forbidden City; others say it was unearthed from an ancient tomb. The Guinness World Records does not verify these origin claims, and the actual history remains uncertain.

How to Use the Chart

The chart works by cross-referencing two variables:

  1. The mother's lunar age at the time of conception (rows)
  2. The lunar month of conception (columns)

The intersection predicts whether the baby will be a boy or a girl. For example, a mother who is 25 in lunar age and conceives in lunar month 3 would look at row 25, column 3 to find the prediction.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using Western age instead of lunar age: A woman who is 28 in Western age might be 29 or 30 in lunar age
  • Using Gregorian months instead of lunar months: Lunar months don't align with January, February, etc.
  • Not accounting for Lunar New Year timing: If conception occurred before Lunar New Year, the mother's lunar age would be different

Scientific Accuracy

Multiple studies have tested the Chinese Gender Prediction Chart against actual birth records:

  • A 1999 study found approximately 50% accuracy - no better than chance
  • A 2010 study in the American Journal of Obstetrics and Gynecology found no predictive value
  • Research from the National Institutes of Health (NIH) confirms that fetal sex is determined by chromosomes at conception, not by calendar dates

Despite its lack of scientific support, the chart remains enormously popular, consulted millions of times each year through pregnancy apps and websites worldwide.

The Science Behind Lunar Calendars

The lunar calendar is based on observable astronomical phenomena. A lunar month (synodic month) is the time between two consecutive new moons: approximately 29.53059 days. This is why lunar months alternate between 29 and 30 days.

A purely lunar year of 12 months is only 354.37 days, about 10.88 days shorter than the solar year (365.25 days). Without correction, the calendar would drift through the seasons over time. The Chinese lunisolar calendar solves this by adding a leap month approximately every 2.7 years (7 leap months in every 19-year cycle, known as the Metonic cycle).

This astronomical precision means the Chinese calendar has accurately tracked both lunar phases and solar seasons for thousands of years, making it one of the most sophisticated traditional calendar systems ever developed.
